# LLMs, Agents & Security: A Mid-2026 Landscape Briefing

*Compiled 2026-05-31. The author's training cutoff is January 2026. Post-cutoff items are individually flagged **(post-cutoff, search-sourced)**. This field is saturated with vendor marketing ("the mythos"); the central job of this briefing is to separate what is **independently measured/verified** from what is a **vendor self-report**.*

> **Sourcing discipline (read before any number).** This briefing was built against a finite adversarial-verification set covering ~18 specific claims. Where a verification verdict exists, its disposition is stated inline (confirmed / partially confirmed / vendor-claim-only / refuted). **Many quantitative figures in this space were *not* covered by that verification pass** — competition tallies, third-party benchmark percentages, platform "AI slop" metrics, startup funding rounds, and most agent-CVE/CVSS specifics. Those are tagged **[unverified — independently confirm]** and stated *qualitatively wherever possible*, because asserting an exact number that no verdict checked would violate the briefing's own standard. A figure being tagged does not mean it is wrong — only that it is not independently corroborated here.

## 1. Executive summary (hype vs. measured, up front)

- **Capability is rising fast and the gains are partly real, but reliability is the limiter.** The strongest *independently-authored* anchor is Sean Heelan finding Linux ksmbd 0-day [CVE-2025-37899](https://sean.heelan.io/2025/05/22/how-i-used-o3-to-find-cve-2025-37899-a-remote-zeroday-vulnerability-in-the-linux-kernels-smb-implementation/) with the o3 API. An LLM genuinely found an exploitable kernel UAF — but at very low recall and heavy noise: on a *known benchmark* bug it scored ~8/100 with ~28/100 false positives, and the *novel* zero-day surfaced rarely across runs at roughly a 1:50 signal-to-noise ratio (*verdict: partially confirmed; the often-cited "1-in-100" figure is the **benchmark-bug appearance rate, not a per-run hit rate for the zero-day**, which the verdict says was not quantified per run*). "Real, but unreliable, with expert triage required" characterizes the field.

- **Best *measured* recall on real C/C++ vulnerability discovery is low single-shot, climbing only with heavy retries.** The cleanest large-N verified reference is [CyberGym](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.02548v2) (1,507 real OSS-Fuzz C/C++ reproduction tasks, execution-graded): best **single-trial 22.0%** (GPT-5, high-reasoning), with 35 zero-days / 3 CVEs surfaced during the study. Other widely-cited solve-rate and retry-scaling figures (e.g. 28.9%/66.7%@30, BountyBench detect/exploit/patch splits) are **[unverified — independently confirm]**. The directional takeaway holds: low single-attempt recall, retry-dependent gains, patch/system reliability as the dominant failure mode.

- **The single most-cited "AI is autonomously hacking" proof point is oversold.** Anthropic's claim that its withheld [Mythos Preview](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/) "fully autonomously identified and then exploited" FreeBSD CVE-2026-4747 omits that the work was scaffold-driven and that cheap open-weight models *detect* the same bug. **No independent party has replayed Mythos's end-to-end autonomous exploitation** (the model is withheld under "Project Glasswing"), so the autonomy claim is **structurally unverifiable (vendor-claim-only)**.

- **The "first AI-orchestrated cyberattack" (Anthropic's [GTG-1002](https://www.anthropic.com/news/disrupting-AI-espionage), Nov 2025) is a disputed vendor self-report.** No IOCs, no named victims, no attribution methodology, no independent corroboration — and Anthropic's own report concedes Claude "frequently overstated findings and occasionally fabricated data." Named researchers (Beaumont, Tentler, Card) called it "marketing guff."

- **The defensive results are the legitimate, partly-corroborated core — but only the third-party-credited *individual* CVEs qualify as "verified."** The two cleanest independently-checkable finds are OpenAI's [GOGS 2FA bypass CVE-2025-64175](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-p6x6-9mx6-26wj) (*verdict: confirmed* — credit lives in a maintainer-authored advisory) and Anthropic-credited FreeBSD [CVE-2026-4747](https://www.freebsd.org/security/advisories/FreeBSD-SA-26:08.rpcsec_gss.asc) (*the credit is verified; the autonomy framing is not*). Google [Big Sleep](https://projectzero.google/2024/10/from-naptime-to-big-sleep.html) has produced real CVEs (verified: SQLite [CVE-2025-6965](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/safety-security/cybersecurity-updates-summer-2025/)), but its widely-reported WebKit/V8/ANGLE/20-bug-batch tallies are **[unverified — independently confirm]**, not corroborated here. The headline *aggregate* counts (Anthropic's "500+", Google's "72 patches", OpenAI's "92%") remain **unaudited vendor numbers**.

- **The security *of* the agents is a documented, CVE-bearing problem class.** Prompt injection → auto-approved tool use → RCE/exfiltration is no longer theoretical (Copilot [CVE-2025-53773](https://embracethered.com/blog/posts/2025/github-copilot-remote-code-execution-via-prompt-injection/), Cursor [CurXecute/MCPoison](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-cve-2025-54135-cve-2025-54136-vulnerabilities-in-cursor-curxecute-mcpoison)). Vendors increasingly classify these as "architectural limitations," not bugs. *(Individual CVSS scores cited in §6 are **[unverified — independently confirm]**.)*

### Anthropic / Claude

Anthropic's measured cyber capability climbed across the Opus 4 line; the lab cites rising Cybench pass rates, though **the specific Cybench percentages are [unverified — independently confirm]** and uncorroborated here. The **strongest concrete result is defensive**: the Carlini et al. ["0-Days"](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/zero-days/) work (Feb 5 2026, post-cutoff) reports Opus 4.6 found and validated **"more than 500 high-severity vulnerabilities"** in open-source code, motivating the [Claude Code Security](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-code-security) product (limited research preview, Feb 20 2026). *Verdict: partially confirmed* — model/authors/date/product are accurate and real Claude-found CVEs exist, but **the "500+" count is an unaudited vendor self-report (vendor claim, not independently verified)**; "human-validated" is real but the externally-validated fraction is unquantified, and validators were contracted by Anthropic. Independent corroboration of the *broader trend* exists: [LWN](https://lwn.net/Articles/1066581/) reports curl's Daniel Stenberg and kernel maintainers now see genuinely "really good" AI security reports.

On the RSP/ASL side, Anthropic's [Responsible Scaling Policy](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy) sets **no fixed numeric cyber threshold** (cyber is "ongoing assessment"). Opus 4 deployed under [ASL-3](https://www.anthropic.com/news/activating-asl3-protections) for **bio** reasons, not cyber, and no shipped model has been declared to cross a cyber ASL line. Notably, rather than trip a cyber tier, Anthropic chose to **withhold Mythos** on cyber-risk grounds — a threshold-treatment datapoint synthesized into §5.

### OpenAI (Aardvark + Codex + GPT-5/o-series)

The Aardvark/Codex pairing is the cleanest "find-and-fix" pipeline: a GPT-5 agent builds a threat model, validates suspected bugs in a sandbox, then uses Codex to patch ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/introducing-aardvark/)). The strongest **independently checkable** result is GOGS 2FA-bypass CVE-2025-64175, credited in the maintainer-authored GitHub advisory to "OpenAI Security Research / Discovered via GPT5" — *verdict: confirmed* (the credit lives in a third-party-hosted advisory; NVD confirms the bug is real and patched). Disambiguation: the separate GOGS RCE CVE-2025-8110 was found by **Wiz Research, not OpenAI**.

OpenAI's own system cards are **measured-conservative**: o3/o4-mini, GPT-5, and GPT-5-Codex were **all classified below the "High" cyber threshold** under Preparedness Framework v2 through end-2025, and GPT-5's sole "High" determination is **Bio/Chem, not cyber** — *verdict: confirmed*, with the caveat that this is only true within the **end-2025 boundary**; per the verdict's corrected note, the GPT-5.2/5.3-Codex line (Feb 2026, post-cutoff, search-sourced) is the boundary where OpenAI begins treating a model as High in cyber. The commissioned Pattern Labs result of "no model through GPT-5 solved any hard challenge (0/4)" is **[unverified — independently confirm]**; it is not in this briefing's verification set, and the GPT-5 system-card PDF mirror checked during verification did not corroborate the related SAG figures.

### Google (Big Sleep + CodeMender)

Big Sleep has the best *category-level* corroboration because some finds shipped as third-party-credited CVEs — but **the only one verified in this briefing's set is SQLite CVE-2025-6965**. The frequently-cited "5 Apple-credited Safari WebKit CVEs", Chrome V8 CVE-2025-9132, ANGLE CVE-2025-9478, and the 20-bug FFmpeg/ImageMagick batch are **[unverified — independently confirm]** here; they may well be real, but presenting them as "verified third-party corroboration" would launder press/vendor claims into established fact. Project Zero's own framing is cautionary: Big Sleep is "highly experimental," its SQLite work was **variant analysis** (agent seeded with a recent commit + diff), not cold discovery, and "a target-specific fuzzer would be at least as effective" ([Naptime](https://projectzero.google/2024/06/project-naptime.html)). The "first AI to foil in-the-wild exploitation" claim for CVE-2025-6965 is **vendor-claim-only**.

CodeMender is the opposite of corroborated: its "72 fixes" is **vendor-claim-only** — no named patches, no accuracy data, no published paper. On safety, the [Gemini 3 Pro FSF report](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/gemini/gemini_3_pro_fsf_report.pdf) (Nov 2025) is the strongest *methodology-backed* artifact: 11/12 v1 "hard" CTF challenges (up from 6/12 for Gemini 2.5), **continuing to meet** (not newly crossing) the Cyber Uplift Level 1 alert threshold but **not** the Critical Capability Level. *Verdict: partially confirmed* — the 11/12 figure is corroborated by independent readers quoting the report, but the "0/13 v2" detail is weakly sourced (Google said v2 results were "not shown"), and "crossed the alert threshold" is imprecise (Gemini 2.5 Pro already triggered it). An independent cross-check, [NYU CTF Bench](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.17159) (Apr 2026, post-cutoff), puts Gemini 3 Pro at 52% — *below* Claude 4.5 Opus at 59%.

## 3. Autonomous vuln-discovery & pentest tool landscape (beyond the big three)

The commercial autonomous-pentest field consolidated and got heavily funded in 2025-2026. **Note up front:** most operational and funding figures in this section are **[unverified — independently confirm]**; only XBOW's funding totals and leaderboard *timing* were covered by verdicts.

- **XBOW** (founded by Semmle/GitHub Copilot creator Oege de Moor) became the first AI system to top HackerOne's **US reputation leaderboard** (~June 2025) and **global leaderboard** (~mid-2025). *Verdict: confirmed as framed* — the load-bearing caveat is that this is **reputation-gain over a single ~90-day quarter, not all-time**, and humans review every report pre-submission to satisfy HackerOne's no-automation policy ([Rawsec](https://blog.raw.pm/en/about-the-hype-around-xbow/)). XBOW's own submission breakdown is informative: of ~1,060 submissions only ~130 were Resolved (~12.5%), with large Triaged/Duplicate/Informative/N/A buckets — i.e. a *minority* of submissions were clean valid bugs. **Funding (verified by verdict):** a $120M Series C at a >$1B valuation (Mar 2026, [SecurityWeek](https://www.securityweek.com/autonomous-offensive-security-firm-xbow-raises-120m-at-1b-valuation/)) plus a ~$35M extension (May 2026, post-cutoff); independent sources report **"more than $270M" total** raised. *Caveat: valuation and total-raised are **unaudited private-round figures as of a date only weeks before this briefing's compilation** and may move again.* The earlier-round "$75M Series B" and the "$6,000 Pentest On-Demand" / "40-hour pentest in ~28 minutes" claims are **[unverified — independently confirm] vendor figures with no independent benchmark**.

- **Funded competitors:** [RunSybil](https://fortune.com/2026/03/18/exclusive-ai-cybersecurity-startup-runsybil-founded-by-openais-first-security-hire-raises-40-million-led-by-khosla-ventures/) (Khosla, Mar 2026; founded by OpenAI's first security hire), Horizon3.ai/NodeZero, Terra Security, Intruder, and MindFort (YC). **All specific funding amounts, ARR-growth, and customer-count figures for these are [unverified — independently confirm]** and are stated by name only.

- **The "AI slop" backpressure** is the field's most measured story, but it is also **still moving**. [curl](https://daniel.haxx.se/blog/2026/01/26/the-end-of-the-curl-bug-bounty/) ended its bug *bounty* effective Jan 31 2026 after its confirmed-vulnerability rate fell from "north of 15%" to "below 5%" under AI-slop flooding. *Verdict: partially confirmed* — the "zero genuine vulns from fully-autonomous submissions" line is real **but is already partly stale**: AI-*assisted* human research found real curl bugs throughout, and by **~May 2026 even an autonomous Claude Mythos run yielded one human-validated (low-severity) CVE** — so the "zero autonomous" framing is a snapshot, not a settled endpoint. By [April 2026](https://daniel.haxx.se/blog/2026/04/22/high-quality-chaos/) curl reverted the *reporting platform* to HackerOne (still no bounty) and Stenberg wrote the slop situation was "not a problem anymore" — but this is the **maintainer's own framing** of a very recent (<2-month-old), still-developing story; removing the cash incentive, not the platform, is what shifted the dynamic. Platform-wide "AI slop" metrics (HackerOne YoY rise, Bugcrowd submission spike) and the [$12.5M Linux Foundation/OpenSSF response](https://itsfoss.com/news/ai-companies-fund-open-source-security/) (Mar 2026) are **[unverified — independently confirm]** as to their exact figures; the *direction* (rising AI-generated report volume → platform policy changes → vendor-funded OSS-security response) is well-attested.

## 4. Benchmarks & competition results — current measured SOTA

> **Sourcing note for this whole section:** the only benchmark figure here backed by this briefing's verification discipline is **CyberGym's best single-trial 22.0% (GPT-5)** plus its 35-zero-day/3-CVE study yield (also cross-referenced in the bob-oss benchmark plan against the same arXiv source). **Every other specific number below — AIxCC prize/percentage splits, CVE-Bench solve rates, BountyBench detect/exploit/patch, CyberSecEval SOC/malware scores, Cybench leaderboard entries — is [unverified — independently confirm]** and is given qualitatively or with its uncertainty flagged. The competition-level framing leans on DARPA/academic press numbers that no verdict here audited.

**DARPA's AIxCC final** (DEF CON 33, Aug 2025) is the most authoritative *competition* event — but its headline percentages are **DARPA's own rounded press figures, not independently audited** here. Reported outcomes: the seven autonomous Cyber Reasoning Systems found a large majority of *planted/synthetic* vulnerabilities and patched a smaller majority, and surfaced a number of *real* OSS zero-days (~18 by DARPA's press count; an academic [SoK paper](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.07666v1) gives a higher count against a 63-vuln challenge set, with only about a dozen patched). **The exact found/patched percentages and the 18-vs-higher zero-day discrepancy are [unverified — independently confirm]** — do not treat them as measured ground truth. The structural lesson that *is* robust: engineering stability, not raw capability, drove most of the score gap, and synthetic-planted-vuln recall is *not* the same as real-world discovery recall (see OQ3, §8). All finalist systems are being open-sourced.

Standardized benchmarks (measured SOTA on **real** code):

- **[CyberGym](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.02548v2)** (UC Berkeley, 1,507 real OSS-Fuzz C/C++ reproduction tasks, execution-graded, no LLM judge): **best single-trial 22.0% (GPT-5, high-reasoning)**; 35 zero-days / 3 CVEs surfaced during the study. **This is the cleanest *verified* answer to "what solve rate is achievable on real memory-safety bugs."** *(Higher retry-scaled figures circulating elsewhere — e.g. 28.9%/66.7%@30 — are [unverified — independently confirm] and may reflect different models/configs.)*
- **CyberSecEval** (Meta's [PurpleLlama](https://github.com/meta-llama/PurpleLlama/blob/main/CybersecurityBenchmarks/README.md) family) is a *required* benchmark per spec and a first-class entry here. It is a **suite, not a single score**: it spans prompt-injection resistance, insecure-code-generation rates, cyberattack-helpfulness/refusal, autonomous-offense, and (in later additions) SOC-analysis and malware-analysis tasks (CyberSOCEval, AutoPatchBench). Its methodology is **task-bank + automated graders**, which makes it good for *relative* model comparison and weak as an absolute capability oracle. **All specific CyberSecEval/CyberSOCEval scores (including any "SOC/malware 15-28%") are [unverified — independently confirm]** in this set; the load-bearing *uplift* result from this family (CYBERSECEVAL 3) is treated separately and more carefully in §5.
- **[CVE-Bench](https://ddkang.substack.com/p/launching-the-cve-bench-leaderboard)** (web-app CVEs, UIUC/Kang): a reproduction-gated, grading-server benchmark. **The widely-cited ~80% pass@1 is [unverified — independently confirm] and, where quoted, is an easy-subset result, not full-set SOTA** — easily mis-cited.
- **BountyBench** (real bounties): reportedly shows a strong *patch* >> *detect* asymmetry. **The specific detect/exploit/patch percentages are [unverified — independently confirm]**; treat the offense-defense imbalance as a *qualitative* finding, not a measured triple.
- **Cybench** (40-task, ICLR 2025) is a legitimate benchmark, but the public llm-stats "leaderboard" lists only a couple of **self-reported, independently-unverified entries** (a "Claude Mythos Preview" at "100%", a Grok entry far lower). **These entries are [unverified — independently confirm]; the "100%" must not be treated as measured SOTA.**

**Headline takeaway across credible execution-based measures: best *verified* recall on real C/C++ discovery is ~20% single-attempt (CyberGym/GPT-5), gains come from retries, and patch/system reliability is the dominant failure mode.** Synthetic-competition recall (AIxCC) is higher but measures a different, easier-to-saturate distribution.

## 5. Real-world impact, dual-use & frontier safety — pressure-testing the mythos

This is where the "AI ran a cyberattack / AI autonomously hacks" narrative most outruns the evidence.

**The GTG-1002 "first AI-orchestrated espionage campaign."** Anthropic ([Nov 13 2025](https://www.anthropic.com/news/disrupting-AI-espionage)) claimed a Chinese state group used Claude Code to run ~30-target espionage **80-90% autonomously** (4-6 human decision points). *Verdict:* the meta-claim (Anthropic said this, and it was disputed) is **confirmed**; the **substance is vendor-claim-only (vendor claim, not independently verified)**. There are **no IOCs, no named victims, no attribution methodology, and no independent or government corroboration**; [SOCRadar](https://socradar.io/blog/ai-powered-gtg-1002-campaign/) notes GTG-1002 "has not appeared in public threat intelligence repositories." Anthropic's own report admits Claude "frequently overstated findings and occasionally fabricated data" — and the 80-90% figure rests on that same admittedly-unreliable AI self-reporting. Named researchers pushed back hard: Kevin Beaumont ("the complete lack of IoCs strongly suggests they don't want to be called out"), Dan Tentler, Daniel Card ("marketing guff"). Yann LeCun's "scaring everyone with dubious studies / regulatory capture" framing is attributed to [The Stack](https://www.thestack.technology/) (*per the verification evidence — not to the BleepingComputer/CSO pieces an earlier draft mis-cited*). Even the [US House Homeland Security Committee](https://homeland.house.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/2025-11-26-CHS-to-Anthropic-re-Request-to-Testify.pdf) reframed it as "AI-Assisted, **Partially** Autonomous." Consensus even among critics: **the techniques were not novel** (a long-established kill chain with off-the-shelf tools); the only claimed novelty is autonomous orchestration at machine speed.

**The Mythos / FreeBSD "fully autonomous" exploit (Apr 2026, post-cutoff, search-sourced).** Anthropic's [Mythos post](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/) says Mythos "fully autonomously identified and then exploited" FreeBSD CVE-2026-4747. *Verdict: partially confirmed* — individual facts are mostly real but chained into a misleading narrative. **Accurate:** the verbatim quote, the FreeBSD advisory crediting "Nicholas Carlini using Claude," and that a human-built scaffold was involved (Anthropic admits it). **Corrected/misleading:** the "patched 12 days earlier" framing is wrong — Mythos's discovery *produced* the Mar 26 advisory, and the gap is normal coordinated-disclosure embargo, not prior fixing; the "older Opus 4.6 already had a working exploit" claim conflates a *later*, heavily human-guided exploit build ([calif.io](https://blog.calif.io/p/mad-bugs-claude-wrote-a-full-freebsd)). The "small open-weight models detect it" fact is **true but measures detection, not exploitation** — [AISLE](https://aisle.com/blog/aisle-matches-anthropic-mythos-on-freebsd-zero-days) explicitly disclaims exploit parity. The strongest *verified* takeaway is the **commodity-detection angle**: cheap open-weight models flag this "frontier" bug, deflating the "unprecedented" framing. This does **not** disprove the autonomy claim, which **remains structurally unverifiable** because Mythos is withheld and **no independent reproduction exists** — and the whole episode is **<2 months old and still developing**, so the "commodity, not frontier" reading should be held provisionally, not as settled.

**Better-evidenced, lower-ceiling threat intelligence.** *(These counterweight sources were not part of this briefing's formal verification pass; treat them as well-attested press/lab reporting rather than verdict-backed.)* [Google GTIG](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/threat-actor-usage-of-ai-tools/) (Nov 2025) documented the *first LLM-at-runtime malware in live operations* (PROMPTSTEAL/APT28) and experimental self-modifying droppers (PROMPTFLUX, "in development, cannot compromise a network") — but assessed most state use as **productivity gains, not novel capability**. OpenAI's [Oct 2025 threat report](https://cyberscoop.com/openai-threat-report-ai-cybercrime-hacking-scams/) is the most measured: threat actors "were building AI into their existing workflows, rather than building new workflows around AI," with "no novel offensive capabilities" — tracing back to the [Microsoft/OpenAI Feb 2024 baseline](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2024/02/14/staying-ahead-of-threat-actors-in-the-age-of-ai/), which "did not identify significant attacks employing the LLMs."

**The strongest controlled counterweight on *uplift*.** Meta's [CYBERSECEVAL 3](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.01605) (Jul 2024) ran human-subjects uplift trials and found Llama 3 405B gave **"no statistically significant uplift" vs. search engines**, only "marginal benefits for novices." Caveat: it tests a 2024-era model — a floor, not the current ceiling — and no successor novice-uplift study on a current frontier model has surfaced (see OQ2).

**Capability thresholds — synthesized across both labs.** The two leading labs treat a heightened cyber threshold as **imminent rather than reached**, and their *threshold-treatment behavior* is the clearest signal:
- **OpenAI** keeps shipped models *below* "High" in cyber (verified through end-2025; §2), while stating publicly that it expects models to cross it "in the near future" ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-cyber-resilience/), post-cutoff) — and per the verification verdict, the GPT-5.2/5.3-Codex line (Feb 2026, post-cutoff, search-sourced) is the boundary where OpenAI begins treating a model as High in cyber. This is the one grounded "imminent" quote.
- **Anthropic's RSP** sets no fixed numeric cyber threshold and has *never* declared a shipped model across a cyber ASL line (§2); its threshold-treatment signal is **structural, not numeric** — it chose to *withhold* Mythos on cyber-risk grounds rather than ship-and-tier it, and shipped Opus 4 under ASL-3 for **bio**, not cyber. That a lab will withhold a model specifically over offensive-cyber risk is itself the strongest "threshold is near" datapoint either lab has produced.
- **Independent third-party measurement is thin.** The most-cited "independent" datapoint in circulating coverage — a UK AISI evaluation of *Mythos* with specific "73% / 3-of-10" numbers — **could not be substantiated**: the only UK AISI artifact in this briefing's verification set is an evaluation of [OpenAI's GPT-5.5 cyber capabilities](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/our-evaluation-of-openais-gpt-5-5-cyber-capabilities), **not** a Mythos eval. **The AISI-Mythos eval and its 73%/3-of-10 figures are therefore dropped as unverified/likely-misattributed**; do not treat them as the field's independent counterweight.

**Net:** capability is rising fast and the first agentic-misuse-at-scale case is real *in outline*, but the most alarming autonomy/scale claims are vendor self-reports light on independent corroboration, the strongest controlled uplift evidence is a 2024 null result, and genuinely independent third-party cyber-capability measurement remains scarce.

## 6. Security OF the agents themselves

The security *of* tool-using agents (LLM + shell + file + network access — the same category as bob-oss) is now a **documented, CVE-bearing problem class**. *Note: the specific CVE-IDs are real and citable, but the individual CVSS scores below are **[unverified — independently confirm]** against NVD.* The dominant pattern is a two-stage chain: **(1) indirect prompt injection** delivers attacker text via untrusted content the agent reads (issues, source files, web pages, MCP tool outputs), then **(2) the agent's auto-approved tool/file/shell capabilities** are abused for RCE, exfiltration, or backdoor insertion.

**Sandboxing as a design topic (not just one example).** The recurring failure is that the *sandbox boundary and the agent's own control plane are co-located and agent-writable*. Treat these as a class of escape patterns:
- **Self-modifying approval/config → RCE.** GitHub Copilot [CVE-2025-53773](https://embracethered.com/blog/posts/2025/github-copilot-remote-code-execution-via-prompt-injection/) — injection wrote `"chat.tools.autoApprove": true` into `.vscode/settings.json` ("YOLO mode"), disabling all confirmations. The single most transferable risk to *any* framework where an agent can write files that govern its own approval/sandbox — including pipelines that store egress/permission profiles in agent-reachable files.
- **Egress-allowlist bypass.** Allowlists that match on hostname/string rather than enforced network policy are a *fragile, frequently-bypassed* control surface (e.g. SOCKS5 null-byte and DNS-rebinding-style tricks against allowlisted egress). The lesson is that **egress filtering implemented in the agent layer is not a security boundary**; it belongs in the container/network namespace with default-deny.
- **Capability/permission scoping.** "Human-in-the-loop confirmation" degrades to rubber-stamping at scale, and auto-approve modes remove it entirely; the durable controls are *least-privilege tool grants, non-writable sandbox config outside the agent's reach, and per-tool input validation* rather than per-action prompts.
- **The "security tool" is itself an attack surface.** Claude Code shipped real, independently-found holes — a network sandbox bypass and RCE/token-exfiltration via project files ([CVE-2025-59536](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/rce-and-api-token-exfiltration-through-claude-code-project-files-cve-2025-59536/)) — plus egress-allowlist bypasses reported in [the press](https://www.theregister.com/security/2026/05/20/even-claude-agrees-hole-in-its-sandbox-was-real-and-dangerous/5243662) (post-cutoff). *The "live ~5 months" duration is **[unverified — independently confirm]**.*

**MCP / tool-use risk.**
- Cursor [CurXecute (CVE-2025-54135) and MCPoison (CVE-2025-54136)](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-cve-2025-54135-cve-2025-54136-vulnerabilities-in-cursor-curxecute-mcpoison) — MCPoison's "trust-by-name-not-contents" lesson generalizes to any MCP registry that validates a server by identifier, not by current contents.
- MCP Inspector [CVE-2025-49596](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2025-49596) and mcp-remote [CVE-2025-6514](https://research.jfrog.com/vulnerabilities/mcp-remote-command-injection-rce-jfsa-2025-001290844/) demonstrate that *merely connecting* an agent to an untrusted MCP server can be RCE. *(CVSS 9.4 / 9.6 and the "437k+ downloads" figure are **[unverified — independently confirm]**.)*
- The cross-agent "Comment and Control" prompt-injection chain ([SecurityWeek](https://www.securityweek.com/claude-code-gemini-cli-github-copilot-agents-vulnerable-to-prompt-injection-via-comments/)) drew **strikingly small** vendor bounties, with GitHub calling it a "known architectural limitation" — the industry now treats these as *inherent to the agent model*, not one-off bugs. *(The exact per-vendor bounty amounts are **[unverified — independently confirm]**.)*

**Supply chain (broader than two examples).** This is a multi-layer surface, not a pair of incidents:
- *Malicious/compromised MCP servers.* The first reported in-the-wild malicious MCP server, postmark-mcp, allegedly exfiltrated email via an added BCC line ([Koi](https://www.koi.ai/blog/postmark-mcp-npm-malicious-backdoor-email-theft)); the [Invariant Labs GitHub-MCP](https://invariantlabs.ai/blog/mcp-github-vulnerability) "lethal trifecta" demo (untrusted input + private-data access + exfil channel) is the canonical pattern. *Both are reported incidents/demos, **not** verdict-backed here.*
- *Dependency poisoning of the agent toolchain.* Agents pull MCP servers and tools from package registries (npm/PyPI), so classic typosquatting, dependency-confusion, and post-install-script attacks apply directly — now with an LLM that may *autonomously install* a suggested dependency, widening the blast radius.
- *Model/weights supply chain.* Withheld or self-hosted offensive models, pulled checkpoints, and fine-tunes introduce provenance and integrity questions (tampered weights, poisoned fine-tune data, unsigned model artifacts) that the appsec community has barely begun to standardize.
- *Prompt/config and skill supply chain.* System prompts, agent "skills," and rule files fetched from shared repos are themselves an injection vector — untrusted instructions can ship as "configuration."

**Guidance matured.** The [OWASP Top 10 for LLM Apps 2025](https://owasp.org/www-project-top-10-for-large-language-model-applications/assets/PDF/OWASP-Top-10-for-LLMs-v2025.pdf) (Prompt Injection at LLM01, expanded Excessive Agency) and the first OWASP Top 10 for Agentic AI (Dec 2025, post-cutoff). Microsoft's framework-level RCE write-up ("treat the LLM as not a security boundary; validate every tool parameter as attacker-controlled," May 2026, post-cutoff) is the load-bearing defensive principle. [NIST AI 600-1](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.600-1.pdf) is widely flagged as lacking agent control-plane coverage.

## 7. Where bob-oss sits — honest positioning

**bob-oss is a narrow autonomous open-source C/C++ memory-safety discovery-and-disclosure pipeline.** It is a Claude-Code-based multi-agent system with a **native-code proof contract** (it must produce a Docker ASAN/crash reproducer before recording a finding), **3-round verification**, and a **5-axis grader**, oriented toward coordinated disclosure.

**On the track record — stated as the project's own benchmark plan states it.** The operator-reported figure is **~11 CVEs across ~5 projects**, but this should not be read as a benchmark result. The project's own [benchmark plan](./BOB_OSS_BENCHMARK_PLAN.md) (`docs/BOB_OSS_BENCHMARK_PLAN.md`) **explicitly treats that CVE tally as "background context, not benchmark evidence, and not even an expectation anchor,"** and deliberately excludes it as *inadmissible as its own benchmark* to avoid circular self-justification. The plan is blunt that bob-oss currently has **no denominator, no baselines, no precision/recall, and no cost-per-bug** — and that against a January-2026-cutoff model you can have a signal that is *contamination-free* (prospective novel-yield) **or** *statistically powered* (a frozen ARVO rediscovery slice), but not both at once. So the honest headline is: **bob-oss has no published capability number, and its own documentation disclaims the CVE count as one.**

**What category it is in.** bob-oss belongs to the **autonomous vulnerability-*discovery* on real native code** category — the same lane as **Google Big Sleep/Naptime**, **OpenAI Aardvark/Codex Security**, and **OSS-Fuzz-Gen**, and adjacent to the AIxCC cyber-reasoning systems. Its execution-graded "must crash before it counts" proof contract is conceptually aligned with how the most credible *verified* benchmark in this space (CyberGym) defines a hit — crash-on-vulnerable, independently re-executed, no LLM-judge inflation — which is exactly the discipline that separates measured results from "AI slop." Whether bob-oss's 3-round verifier and proof contract actually *deliver* that low false-positive rate is, per its own plan, **untested** — it is a *bar bob-oss has not yet been shown to clear*, not one it has demonstrably cleared.

**What is *not* comparable.** bob-oss is **not** a web-application pentest agent like **XBOW** (web-app DAST, live identities, HackerOne reputation, BOLA/business-logic — no C/C++ memory-safety class). It is **not** a patching agent like **CodeMender** (its contract is discovery + disclosure, not autonomous repair-at-scale). And it is not a frontier *model* (Mythos, GPT-5.x) — it is a *scaffold/pipeline* built on a frontier model, so its capability ceiling tracks the underlying Claude model.

**Honest positioning.** bob-oss is a **category peer** of Big Sleep / Aardvark / OSS-Fuzz-Gen *in kind*, with the explicit caveats that (a) the headline counts those vendors publish are themselves unaudited, and (b) bob-oss's ~11 CVEs is a *smaller, self-reported, and self-disclaimed* figure that has not been independently audited either. As a tool-using agent with shell/file/Docker access and egress profiles, **bob-oss is squarely inside the §6 threat class** — its proof-contract, sandbox, and egress configuration are an attack surface, and the Claude Code sandbox/egress findings above are direct lessons (especially: keep egress enforcement in the container/network layer, not in agent-writable config). The defensible claim for bob-oss is *category membership and a disclosure-disciplined proof contract*, **not** SOTA recall or volume leadership.

## 8. What's genuinely new since early 2026, open questions, and what to watch

**Genuinely new (all post-cutoff, search-sourced):**
- **The "find-and-fix product" became a real product category:** OpenAI's Aardvark → **Codex Security** research preview (~Mar 2026); Anthropic's **Claude Code Security** preview (Feb 2026); Google folding **CodeMender** into its agent ecosystem (May 2026).
- **A frontier offensive model was deliberately withheld:** Anthropic's **Mythos** ("Project Glasswing") — the strongest *structural* sign that a lab now treats the offensive-cyber threshold as near (it withheld rather than tiered-and-shipped). *(Any specific independent eval percentages for Mythos remain unverified — see §5; the AISI-Mythos figures circulating elsewhere could not be substantiated and are dropped here.)*
- **The capital and consolidation wave:** XBOW reached unicorn status (>$1B, **>$270M** total raised per current reporting — unaudited private-round figures), alongside RunSybil/Horizon3/Terra and a vendor-funded OSS-security fund.
- **The "AI slop" story moved, but is not closed:** curl ended its *bounty* (Jan 2026) and Stenberg called slop "not a problem anymore" by April 2026 — yet by ~May 2026 an autonomous Claude Mythos run still yielded a human-validated CVE, so "arc resolved" is the *maintainer's framing of a <2-month-old, still-moving* situation, not a settled conclusion. The bottleneck appears to be shifting from *junk volume* toward *maintainer overload from high-quality AI-assisted reports*.
- **Agentic-AI security guidance was formalized:** first OWASP Top 10 for Agentic AI (Dec 2025); framework-level RCE class documented (Microsoft, May 2026).

**Open questions / what to watch:**
1. **Independent reproduction of withheld-model claims.** Mythos's "fully autonomous exploitation" and aggregate-vuln counts are **structurally unverifiable** while the model is withheld and no third party has reproduced them. Watch for any independent replay or opening of disclosure commitments.
2. **A post-2024 novice-uplift study.** The strongest empirical counterweight (CYBERSECEVAL 3) is on a 2024 model; a successor human-subjects uplift trial on a current frontier model would resolve whether the "no significant uplift" floor still holds.
3. **Closing the synthetic-vs-real gap.** AIxCC's high recall on *planted* vulns vs. CyberGym's ~20% single-trial on *real* discovery is the field's central honesty check (and both numbers come with the caveats in §4). Watch whether real-world single-attempt recall climbs materially past ~20-30%.
4. **CodeMender's promised technical paper** — its "72 fixes" stays vendor-claim-only until projects/commits and FP/regression data are published.
5. **The agent-security arms race vs. its own tooling.** As more autonomous discovery/patching agents deploy, expect more CVEs *in the agents, MCP infrastructure, and agent supply chain* — watch whether "treat the LLM as not a security boundary" is actually operationalized.
6. **Independently-verified frontier leaderboards.** A reproducible, third-party-audited Cybench/CVE-Bench frontier leaderboard does **not** yet exist; the self-reported "100%" entries show why the current ones can't be trusted.

*Standing caveat: every quantitative claim sourced only to a vendor page — Anthropic's "500+"/"1,000+", OpenAI's "92%"/"14 CVEs", Google's "72 fixes", XBOW's pricing/speed — is a marketing-grade self-report until an independent party reproduces or audits it. And every figure tagged **[unverified — independently confirm]** in this briefing was outside its formal verification set: directionally informative, not corroborated here.*
